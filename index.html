Extrinsic Noise in Gene Expression

Ziqi Lu     CID: 01043013

Supervisors: Rowan Brackston, John Pinney


Abstract

Gene expression is intrinsically stochastic and the event of mRNA production has been found to occur in bursts. This bursty transcription is described by a telegraph model, but it is not sufficient to account for certain features in the mRNA copy number distribution such as the heavy-tailedness. A compound model that draws telegraph model rate parameter from a lognormal distribution incorporates extrinsic noise, and has been analytically proven to generate heavy-tailed distributions. In this project with the two mathematical models available, I use the MCMC method to fit the models to online single cell RNA-seq data, infer model parameters, and apply a Bayes factor derived method for model comparison. With these methods I manage to find a group of genes preferring extrinsic noise model, yet a best model may require mixing of the two.



Lay Summary

Understanding the machinery of transcription is essential for untangling the complicated network of gene regulation and downstream protein production. The process of transcription is inherently a noisy process. These noise mainly root from the transcription machinery, and contribute to cell-to-cell differences in the measured mRNA copy numbers. However, there is another source of noise called the extrinsic noise, which is brought by environmental factors external to the transcription machinery such as the number RNA polymerase present in a cell. With the mathematical models for transcription with and without extrinsic noise available, I am able to use statistical inference to study the existence of extrinsic noise using data from online resources, as well as investigate into the details of these model fitting.
